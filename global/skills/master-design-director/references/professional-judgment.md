## Purpose

Use this reference to diagnose whether work is merely attractive or professionally authored. Apply it to taste calibration, false luxury detection, optical judgment, premium leaks, and the "almost good but not resolved" state.

## Professional Judgment

Treat professional design judgment as the ability to perceive a layout as a living system of meaning, structure, optical force, and emotional sequence. Do not judge isolated components first. Judge relationships.

Ask:

- What is the governing visual thesis?
- What is the first, second, and third read?
- What does the viewer feel, notice, and believe?
- What is structural, and what is costume?
- What would remain if decorative finish, beautiful photography, and trend styling disappeared?

Call out attractive work that lacks authorship. Say: "This is attractive, but not resolved. It has polish without a governing thesis."

## Beginner Eye vs Senior Eye

| Area | Beginner Eye | Senior Eye | Director Action |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Alignment | Trusts tool centering and bounding boxes | Corrects for optical weight, density, side bearings, and image mass | Flag false centering and ask whether the element is visually or mathematically centered |
| Whitespace | Treats space as padding or absence | Treats negative space as load-bearing structure | Ask what job the silence performs |
| Hierarchy | Makes many things louder to avoid missed information | Forces a strict focal path and lets secondary elements recede | Demand one primary read per scene |
| Typography | Chooses type for style and leaves defaults | Manages typographic color, measure, leading, tracking, and role | Audit type as texture and architecture |
| Grid | Places locally attractive elements | Reads the invisible structural net across sections | Trace anchor lines and identify arbitrary drift |
| Imagery | Selects beautiful images | Selects images for narrative utility, depth, crop, proof, and sequence | Ask what each image communicates that copy cannot |
| Motion | Adds energy and premium feeling | Choreographs attention, threshold, materiality, and time | Reject motion that does not change understanding |

## Authorship vs Polish

Authored work has a governing thesis that dictates every micro-decision. It does not try to please every content block equally. It forces a perspective.

Signs of authorship:

- A clear visual thesis can be stated in one sentence.
- Typography, image logic, grid behavior, motion, and silence repeat with intention.
- The page alternates tension and release.
- The brand could not be swapped with a competitor without loss.
- Subtraction has removed weak, decorative, and redundant elements.
- Dense proof moments are balanced by structural silence.

Signs of polished but unauthored work:

- It looks expensive in fragments but generic as a system.
- It relies on premium templates, beige/black palettes, large spaces, fashionable type, or cinematic imagery without a distinct thesis.
- Individual sections look good, but the scroll feels episodic.
- The grid changes by convenience.
- The hero feels atmospheric but does not establish authority.
- The page is hard to criticize technically, yet still feels unresolved.

Use this line when needed: "This looks expensive in fragments, but not authored as a system."

## Optical Sensitivity

Audit optical reality before numeric correctness.

- Mathematical centering may feel low, off-axis, or visually skewed.
- Display type side bearings can create false margin alignment.
- Large type may need optical margin correction.
- Dense imagery may require more breathing distance than light imagery.
- Thin type may appear weaker than its CSS value suggests.
- Image crops can destroy vanishing points, depth, furniture logic, or spatial continuity.

Questions:

- If blurred or viewed at thumbnail scale, where does visual weight collect?
- What element holds the darkest typographic color?
- What alignment feels almost correct but not quiet?
- Which space has no shape, rhythm, or tension?
- What tangent or near-tangent flattens depth?

## Typographic Color

Treat text blocks as visual material before treating them as words. Typographic color is the perceived darkness, density, or lightness of type on the page.

Audit:

- Does the body text feel too dark, timid, airy, cramped, or disconnected from adjacent imagery?
- Does display type form a confident mass, or does it fall apart through weak weight, excessive tracking, or poor line breaks?
- Is delicate type supported by strong grid, contrast, and negative space?
- Does the type command, narrate, annotate, punctuate, or pause?
- Is text measured for reading comfort, or stretched to fill layout?

Use numeric rules as practical heuristics, not universal law. A 45-75 character measure, generous body leading, and extreme display/body contrast are useful checks, but context, language, font, viewport, and intent decide the final judgment.

## False Luxury And Premium Leaks

| Pattern | Failure Sign | Director Response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gold without material logic | Gold buttons, lines, gradients, or accents appear because "gold means luxury" | "Gold without material logic is costume. What physical or brand material justifies it?" |
| Generic warm minimalism | Beige, taupe, cream, and black appear without texture, contrast, or brand specificity | "This is quiet, but not authored. What makes this palette belong to this studio?" |
| Thin type without structure | Delicate serif or light sans appears fragile, low contrast, or unsupported | "The type is delicate, but the page structure is too weak to support delicacy." |
| Excessive whitespace | Large gaps do not frame, group, pause, or guide | "The whitespace is large, but not intelligent." |
| Cinematic imagery without narrative | Beautiful image has mood but no proof, transformation, or specificity | "The image is beautiful, but it is not doing work." |
| Low-contrast UI pretending to be refined | CTA, nav, or body copy is hard to read | "Weak contrast is not refinement. It is indecision." |
| Pseudo-editorial layout | Asymmetry, overlap, and columns imitate magazines without grid logic | "You are imitating editorial form, not editorial structure." |
| Stock-like interiors | Images feel generic, over-polished, or disconnected from project specificity | "This could belong to any studio. Where is the spatial authorship?" |
| Empty luxury language | Elevated, timeless, bespoke, premium appear without proof | "The copy is declaring luxury because the design has not demonstrated it." |
| Motion as drama | Animation compensates for weak hierarchy | "The motion is decorating a weak transition." |

## Almost Good But Not Resolved

Use this diagnostic when a design has no obvious technical failure but still lacks professional authority.

| Axis | Test | Unresolved Symptom |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hierarchy | Can the eye path be mapped without ambiguity? | Eye bounces between logo, image, headline, and CTA |
| Grid Logic | Does negative space reveal structure when content is mentally removed? | Elements float or break grid without narrative reason |
| Typographic Relationship | Does type balance the image weight? | Captions disappear, display feels timid, body feels heavy |
| Image-Copy Breathing | Do image and text have enough separation to converse? | Copy clings to image or repeats what image already says |
| Pacing | Does scroll alternate density and release? | Sections create a monotonous drumbeat |
| Emotional Sequence | Does desire build before action is requested? | CTA interrupts atmosphere or proof |
| Brand Authority | Could a competitor logo fit without friction? | Generic luxury template signals dominate |

Verdict language:

- "This is not wrong, but it is unresolved."
- "The work is polished, but the hierarchy remains beginner-level."
- "The composition has atmosphere, but no authority."
- "The design is quiet, but not confident."

## Taste Calibration Practices

Use these exercises to train the user's eye instead of merely supplying answers.

1. Grey Blur Test: Remove color and detail mentally. Identify the darkest mass and first read.
2. Reference Decomposition: Break admired work into grid, spacing, typography, image roles, and silence before copying surface style.
3. Constraint Redesign: Solve hierarchy using only spacing and scale; then using only weight and position; then using only subtraction.
4. Subtraction Drill: Remove one decorative element and ask what becomes stronger.
5. Anti-Example Library: Collect attractive-but-unresolved designs and label the failure precisely.
6. Thumbnail Test: Shrink the layout and ask what survives.
7. Sequence Test: Ask a viewer what they remember first, second, and third after three seconds.
8. Context Test: Place the work beside premium competitors and ask what feels authored versus assembled.

## Senior Questions

- What is the governing visual idea, and can you state it without using "premium", "minimal", or "luxury"?
- What should the viewer believe after this section that they did not believe before?
- What would be lost if this image disappeared?
- What is the weakest element on this page?
- Which spacing decisions are structural and which are leftovers?
- Where are you using polish to avoid making a harder decision?
- Which element is loudest, and should it be?
- Where does the design become generic enough that another studio could wear it?

## Anti-Hallucination Rules

- Do not invent exact brand measurements, typefaces, CSS values, or material systems without evidence.
- Use proportion systems, golden-ratio references, canons, and grid ratios as optional diagnostic lenses, not immutable laws.
- Prefer observable failures over mystical design rules.
- If evidence is missing, say what must be inspected: screenshot, prototype, assets, audience, brand thesis, or page sequence.
