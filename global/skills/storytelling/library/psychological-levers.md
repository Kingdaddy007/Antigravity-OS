The mental mechanisms that make storytelling work. Each lever is a tool
for capturing and holding attention.

---

## INFORMATION ASYMMETRY

**What it does:** User knows less than the system. Creates curiosity.
**How it works:** Show enough to intrigue, withhold enough to compel.
**When to use:** Mystery, data platforms, hidden layers, exclusive access.
**Web translation:**
- Reveal content progressively on scroll
- Use clip-path or mask to partially hide content
- Show previews that require interaction to unlock
- Use depth (z-index, 3D layers) to create "what's behind" tension

**Example:** The City & The City — two overlapping realities, user can only see one at a time.

---

## CURIOSITY GAP

**What it does:** Something is withheld. User wants to know.
**How it works:** Create a question in the user's mind that demands an answer.
**When to use:** All storytelling — this is the fundamental hook.
**Web translation:**
- Headlines that pose questions or make bold claims
- Visual elements that are partially visible
- Scroll reveals that answer questions progressively
- CTAs that promise to reveal more

**Example:** The Silent Patient — "She hasn't spoken in years." Why?

---

## PATTERN VIOLATION

**What it does:** Something breaks expectations. Creates surprise.
**How it works:** Establish a pattern, then break it at the right moment.
**When to use:** Experimental, creative, disruptive brands.
**Web translation:**
- Consistent layout that suddenly changes
- Expected behavior that doesn't happen
- Visual elements that move in unexpected ways
- Typography that breaks conventions

**Example:** Interior Chinatown — novel formatted as a screenplay, breaking expectations.

---

## STATUS THREAT

**What it does:** User feels vulnerable or challenged.
**How it works:** Create a gap between where the user is and where they want to be.
**When to use:** Luxury (aspirational), coaching (transformation), fitness (goals).
**Web translation:**
- Show the "before" state (what the user is now)
- Show the "after" state (what they could be)
- Use second-person copy ("You are here")
- Create aspiration through imagery and tone

**Example:** Open Water — "You walk into the bar." Second-person places you inside the experience.

---

## DREAD ANTICIPATION

**What it does:** Something bad is coming. Creates tension.
**How it works:** Build slowly toward a payoff. The waiting is the experience.
**When to use:** Atmospheric, horror, slow-burn brands, suspense.
**Web translation:**
- Slow scroll pacing with long reveals
- Gradual environmental changes (color shifts, fog density)
- Countdown timers or progress indicators
- Elements that degrade or change over time

**Example:** Fever Dream — "We don't have much time." Evaporating timeline creates dread.

---

## LOSS AVERSION

**What it does:** User fears losing something. Creates urgency.
**How it works:** Frame the situation as a potential loss, not a potential gain.
**When to use:** Conversion, limited offers, scarcity, urgency.
**Web translation:**
- Limited-time offers with countdowns
- "Only X left" messaging
- Progress bars that show completion
- Exit-intent popups with offers

**Example:** Never Let Me Go — the characters' fate is sealed, creating loss aversion.

---

## PATTERN-MATCHING COMPULSION

**What it does:** User must assemble fragments. Creates engagement.
**How it works:** Give the user pieces that they need to put together.
**When to use:** Complex narratives, multi-section pages, puzzle-like experiences.
**Web translation:**
- Scattered elements that form a picture when assembled
- Progressive reveals that build understanding
- Interactive elements that require exploration
- Hidden connections between sections

**Example:** Cloud Atlas — six stories across eras, user must find the connections.

---

## SOCIAL PROOF

**What it does:** Others validate the experience. Creates trust.
**How it works:** Show that others have trusted, tried, and succeeded.
**When to use:** Testimonials, case studies, community brands, conversion.
**Web translation:**
- Customer testimonials with photos
- Case studies with numbers
- User counts ("Join 10,000+")
- Press logos and awards

**Example:** Lincoln in the Bardo — 166 voices testify, creating overwhelming social proof.
